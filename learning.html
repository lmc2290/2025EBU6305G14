Introduction to Compression

Compression is the process of encoding information using fewer bits than the original representation. It's a fundamental concept in digital technology, allowing us to store and transmit data more efficiently. Think of it as packing a suitcase — you want to fit as much as possible while taking up the least amount of space.

Why is Compression Important?

In today's digital world, we deal with vast amounts of data, from high-resolution images and videos to complex software. Without compression, storing and sharing this data would be incredibly slow and expensive. Compression helps in:

  • Reducing storage space on devices and servers.
  • Speeding up data transmission over networks (e.g., faster website loading).
  • Lowering bandwidth requirements.

How Does it Work?

At its core, compression works by identifying and removing redundant information. For example, in an image, if a large area is the same color, instead of storing the color information for every single pixel in that area, compression algorithms can store the color once and note how many times it repeats.
